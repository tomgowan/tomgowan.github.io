About

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Atmospheric Sciences (expected graduation fall 2020). I have a strong background in numerical weather prediction, machine learning, large-eddy simulations, distributed computing, ensembles, uncertainty quantification, probabilistic validation, precipitation processes, boundary layer and mountain meteorology.

My MS research involved working with and validating high-resolution, ensemble numerical weather prediction systems. The first half of my PhD research focused on modeling lake-effect and orographic precipitation in idealized large-eddy simulations. Currently, I'm working on developing a lake-effect prediction system that involves identifying lake-effect periods in operational weather models and then downscaling and enhancing the spatial forecasts using deep learning models, such as Fully Convolutional Networks (FCNs) and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs).

Current Activities

ML-Enhanced Lake-Effect Forecasts

The second half of my Ph.D. research focuses on developing a lake-effect prediction system that involves identifying lake-effect periods in operational weather models and then downscaling and enhancing the spatial forecasts using image-to-image translation deep learning techniques, such as Fully Convolutional Networks (FCNs) and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The example shown here includes a (a) raw 12-h HRRR forecast, (b) an ML-Enhanced 12- h HRRR forecast trained using the pix2pix image-to-image translation deep learning model, and (c) the MRMS precipitation analyses for the valid forecast time.

Idealized Lake-Effect Modeling

The first half of my Ph.D. research involves designing, conducting, and analyzing idealized large-eddy simulations of banded and cellular lake-effect precipitation interacting with downstream terrain. We seek to understand how the precipitation distributions of these two modes are affected by varied orography and coastlines.
